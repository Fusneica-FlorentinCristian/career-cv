# CV Standard

A reusable standard for generating clean, ATS-friendly, role-targeted CVs for any candidate.

This document is meant to be the **source-of-truth specification** behind future CV variants, whether they are exported to HTML, PDF, Word, or Markdown.

---

## 1. Purpose

Use this standard to generate CVs that are:

- easy to skim in 10–15 seconds
- credible and fact-based
- optimized for recruiters and ATS systems
- visually clean in print/PDF
- easy to adapt per role, seniority, and candidate profile

This standard is strongest for:

- software / IT / engineering CVs
- product / operations / project / analytical roles
- most professional white-collar CVs

For highly creative roles, portfolio-heavy roles, or academic CVs, use this as the base and extend only where necessary.

---

## 2. Signature

The signature of the CV style should be:

> **quiet, sharp, structured, and credible**

It should feel like:

- editorial, not templated
- modern, not flashy
- technical/professional, not decorative
- confident, not exaggerated

If the design calls attention to itself before the content does, it is too loud.

---

## 3. Core principles

1. **Truth over theater**  
   Every claim must be defensible.

2. **One target per version**  
   A CV should target one primary role family at a time.

3. **One page by default**  
   Use two pages only when the candidate genuinely has enough relevant material.

4. **Skimmable first, detailed second**  
   The recruiter should understand the candidate’s fit in seconds.

5. **Impact beats task lists**  
   Bullets should describe outcomes, systems, and ownership—not just duties.

6. **Restraint beats gimmicks**  
   No charts, stars, progress bars, or decorative widgets.

7. **Content drives layout**  
   The design must support the hierarchy of information, not compete with it.

---

## 4. Page-count rules

### Default

- **0–10 years experience:** 1 page
- **10+ years experience:** 1–2 pages depending on relevance
- **Executive / deep leadership / academic:** 2 pages maximum unless a true CV format is required

### Expand to 2 pages only if at least one is true

- the candidate has substantial senior-level leadership scope
- the candidate has multiple highly relevant employers/projects
- the candidate has certifications/publications/projects that materially improve interview conversion
- compressing to one page would remove important evidence of fit

### Never expand to 2 pages just because

- the candidate has many old roles
- the candidate wants to list every tool ever touched
- the candidate wants paragraphs instead of concise bullets

---

## 5. Structural standard

### Default layout

Use a **two-column print-first layout**.

#### Sidebar / rail

Use for compact, high-signal reference info:

- contact
- location
- links
- best-fit / target roles
- core skills or stack
- strengths
- languages
- certifications

#### Main column

Use for the narrative and proof:

1. profile / summary
2. experience
3. education
4. optional projects / awards / publications (only if relevant)

### Order rules

- Put the most decision-driving information first.
- For mid-level and senior candidates, experience should dominate.
- For juniors, education/projects may move higher if they provide stronger evidence.

---

## 6. Design rules

### Page and export

- A4 by default
- print-first spacing
- should export cleanly to PDF
- no layout that depends on hover, animation, or interactivity

### Visual style

- use **one primary ink/base color**
- use **one restrained accent color**
- avoid gradients, glows, heavy shadows, decorative frames, and novelty effects
- use lines/rules sparingly to separate sections

### Typography

- prioritize readability over personality
- clear hierarchy for:
  - name
  - role/title
  - section headers
  - company/role/date
  - body text
- avoid overly condensed, handwritten, or display-heavy fonts

### Photos and icons

- **no photo by default**
- include a photo only when culturally expected and not harmful to ATS/export quality
- icons are optional and should remain subtle

### Things to avoid

- skill bars
- skill dots/stars
- circular percentage widgets
- multi-color palette explosions
- large decorative headers
- copy-to-clipboard widgets
- visible template labels or internal notes

---

## 7. Content rules

### Name and headline

The top of the CV must immediately answer:

- who is this?
- what role are they targeting?
- what kind of professional are they?

#### Good headline examples

- DevOps Engineer · Platform / CI/CD / Build Systems
- Backend Engineer · C# / .NET / APIs
- Product Manager · B2B SaaS / Platform Products
- Finance Analyst · FP&A / Reporting / Forecasting

Avoid headlines that are too broad, vague, or generic.

---

### Summary / profile

Use **3–5 lines max**.

The summary should establish:

- role identity
- years of experience or career stage
- strongest domains/tools
- what the person is especially good at
- what roles they fit best

#### Summary formula

`[Role identity] with [X years / career stage] in [domain]. Strongest in [strength 1], [strength 2], and [strength 3]. Best suited for [target role family / problem space].`

#### Example

`Platform engineer with 6 years in CI/CD, internal tooling, and cloud delivery systems. Strongest in automation design, release reliability, and developer workflow improvement. Best suited for platform, DevOps, and build/release engineering roles.`

---

### Experience

This is the core proof section.

Each role should contain:

- title
- company
- dates
- optional context/subtitle
- optional stack line
- 2–4 bullets

#### Bullet formula

Each bullet should ideally contain:

- **action**
- **system / scope**
- **outcome / effect**

Formula:

`[Verb] [thing/system/process] using [relevant tools/approach], resulting in [impact/outcome].`

#### Good bullet examples

- Improved release reliability by consolidating deployment steps into reusable Jenkins pipelines and reducing manual failures.
- Built reporting workflows in Power BI and SQL that shortened monthly reporting turnaround for finance stakeholders.
- Led onboarding documentation and training for new engineers, reducing ramp-up friction across the team.

#### Bullet count rules

- major recent roles: **3–4 bullets**
- smaller/recent roles: **2–3 bullets**
- internships / older roles: **1–2 bullets**

#### Role weighting

- give more space to the most relevant and recent experience
- compress older or less relevant roles
- omit irrelevant experience if it weakens the narrative

---

### Skills / stack

Skills should be grouped, not dumped randomly.

Recommended grouping:

- languages
- frameworks/platforms
- cloud/devops/tools
- data/analytics tools
- methods/domain strengths

#### Good example

- **Languages:** Python, C#, SQL
- **Cloud & DevOps:** Azure, Jenkins, Docker, GitHub Actions
- **Frameworks:** .NET, React, Django
- **Strengths:** CI/CD design, automation, troubleshooting, onboarding

#### Avoid

- giant keyword walls in visible sections
- fake proficiency levels unless they are genuinely standardized
- rating systems (e.g. 4/5, stars, dots, bars)

---

### Education

Include:

- degree or certification name
- institution
- dates (optional if not useful)
- thesis/honors only if relevant or strong

For senior candidates, education should stay compact.
For junior candidates, it can carry more weight.

---

### Optional sections

Use only when they help the target role:

- projects
- publications
- awards
- volunteering
- speaking
- open-source contributions

If a section does not improve interview odds, cut it.

---

## 8. Writing rules

### Tone

The tone should be:

- direct
- competent
- specific
- calm
- evidence-based

### Always do

- use strong verbs
- use numbers when defensible
- prefer concrete nouns over abstract claims
- compress repetition
- tailor keywords to the role

### Never do

- write in first person
- use buzzword soup
- exaggerate scope
- call someone “expert” without proof
- use full paragraphs for experience bullets
- use vague filler like “hard-working,” “team player,” or “passionate” unless backed by evidence

---

## 9. ATS rules

The CV must remain ATS-friendly.

### Required

- real text, not image-based text
- consistent section names
- selectable/copyable content
- clean date formatting
- clear role and company names
- standard contact fields

### Recommended

- role-relevant keywords naturally embedded in summary, skills, and bullets
- an optional hidden keyword block if using HTML output and it does not harm readability

### Avoid

- putting important content only in graphics
- overly unusual section names
- complex visual dependency for core information

---

## 10. Adaptation rules by profile type

### Junior / early-career

Emphasize:

- education
- internships
- projects
- strong transferable skills
- practical evidence of capability

Reduce:

- abstract summary claims
- bloated tools lists

### Mid-level

Emphasize:

- ownership
- systems touched
- delivery impact
- team collaboration
- specialization direction

### Senior / lead

Emphasize:

- scope
- architecture/strategy
- mentoring/leadership
- organizational impact
- reliability/cost/risk decisions

### Career switcher

Emphasize:

- transferable wins
- target-role framing
- selected relevant experience only
- projects/certifications that support the transition

### Non-technical professional

Keep the same structure, but translate sections accordingly:

- tools -> business systems/platforms
- stack -> functional competencies
- projects -> initiatives/programs
- outcomes -> revenue, cost, efficiency, service, compliance, customer, process impact

---

## 11. Generation workflow

Use this sequence every time.

### Step 1: collect source facts

Gather the raw candidate information before writing:

- name
- contact details
- location
- links
- target roles
- years of experience
- work history
- key projects
- measurable wins
- education
- certifications
- languages
- strengths
- things that should **not** be claimed

### Step 2: define the target version

Choose:

- role family
- seniority level
- geography/language if relevant
- whether this is a general version or a role-specific version

### Step 3: build the narrative

Decide:

- what the candidate should be remembered for
- which roles should dominate
- what strengths belong in the summary

### Step 4: write summary and experience

- write the summary last or second-last
- write bullets around evidence, not responsibilities
- trim older roles aggressively

### Step 5: fit the page

- compress low-value text
- combine repetitive bullets
- shorten stack lines
- move extra evidence to LinkedIn/GitHub/portfolio if needed

### Step 6: ATS and export check

- verify keywords match the target role
- verify the PDF prints cleanly
- verify the top third of the page communicates fit instantly

## 13. Quality gate

Before considering a CV done, confirm all of these:

- Is the target role obvious in under 5 seconds?
- Is the candidate’s strongest value visible in the top third?
- Is the CV one page unless there is a strong reason not to be?
- Are the bullets specific and outcome-oriented?
- Are the claims fully defensible?
- Is the most relevant experience given the most space?
- Are the keywords aligned with the actual target job?
- Does the exported PDF look clean and print-ready?
- Does the design feel restrained rather than decorative?

If any answer is “no,” revise before exporting.

---

## 14. Default standard summary

When in doubt, use this default:

- **1 A4 page**
- **two-column layout**
- **restrained palette**
- **no gimmicks**
- **clear target headline**
- **short summary**
- **2–4 bullets per role**
- **skills grouped as text**
- **ATS-safe content**
- **only defensible claims**

That is the baseline standard for future CV generation.
